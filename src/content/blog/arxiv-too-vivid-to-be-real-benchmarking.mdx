## The Gap

Text-to-image models like DALL-E and Stable Diffusion have reached impressive visual quality, but there's a subtle problem: when you ask them to generate "realistic" photos, they produce images that look too polished, too saturated, too contrasty. They don't look like photos from a camera—they look like photos that went through aggressive Instagram filters.

The root cause isn't the models themselves, but how we evaluate them. Human raters and preference-trained metrics (like CLIP-based scores) systematically favor vivid, punchy images over authentic ones. This creates a feedback loop: models are trained to maximize these metrics, so they learn to oversaturate. Prior work focused on overall image quality or semantic accuracy, but nobody systematically addressed color fidelity—the gap between synthetic color distributions and real photography.

## The Increment

**One sentence**: Before this paper, we had no objective way to measure color realism in generated images; after it, we have a dataset, a learned metric, and a refinement method that together form a closed loop for assessing and improving color fidelity.

### Core Mechanism

The system has three interlocking pieces. First, the Color Fidelity Dataset (CFD) contains 1.3M images arranged in ordered levels of color realism—from real photos to progressively more oversaturated synthetic images. This creates a training curriculum where the model learns what "too vivid" means.

Second, the Color Fidelity Metric (CFM) is a multimodal encoder trained on CFD to predict color realism scores. It learns attention patterns that highlight regions where color deviates from photographic norms—oversaturated skies, unnaturally vibrant foliage, etc. Unlike CLIP or FID, CFM specifically targets color distribution rather than semantic content or overall quality.

Third, Color Fidelity Refinement (CFR) is a training-free technique that modulates the guidance scale during diffusion sampling. The key insight: guidance scale controls how strongly the model follows the text prompt, but high guidance amplifies color saturation. CFR adaptively reduces guidance in spatial-temporal regions where CFM detects color deviation, letting the model drift toward more natural color distributions without losing semantic fidelity.

```
Real photos -----> [CFD Dataset] -----> Ordered color realism levels

```

Think of it like a photo editor with three components. CFD is your reference library—thousands of photos showing the spectrum from "straight out of camera" to "heavily edited." CFM is your trained eye that can instantly spot when colors look "off"—it's learned what real camera sensors capture versus what overzealous editing produces. CFR is your selective adjustment tool—instead of globally desaturating the whole image (which would make it dull), it identifies specific regions and moments during generation where the model is "pushing too hard" on the saturation slider, and it gently pulls back just in those spots. The metaphor holds because each component maps directly: the reference library trains the eye, the eye guides the adjustment tool, and the tool operates selectively rather than globally.

### Key Concepts

- **Color Fidelity vs Visual Quality**: These are orthogonal dimensions. An image can be high quality (sharp, well-composed, semantically accurate) but low fidelity (colors don't match real photography). Think of a movie poster versus a documentary still—both can be "good" images, but only one aims for photographic authenticity. Existing metrics conflate these dimensions, rewarding vividness as if it were quality. This paper decouples them: you can have a beautiful, vivid fantasy scene (high quality, low fidelity) or a mundane, authentic street photo (high fidelity, possibly lower aesthetic appeal). The key insight is that for "realistic-style" prompts, we want high fidelity, but current models optimize for vividness instead.

- **Spatial-Temporal Guidance Modulation**: Standard diffusion models use a single guidance scale for the entire image and entire denoising process.
