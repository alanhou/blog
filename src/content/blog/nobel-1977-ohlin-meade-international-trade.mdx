## Why Does Brazil Export Coffee and Japan Export Cars?

Before Ohlin, the dominant answer was Ricardo's: countries trade because they're better at making different things — comparative advantage driven by differences in technology. But Ohlin asked a deeper question: *why* are countries better at different things? His answer shifted the entire foundation of trade theory: it's not about technology, it's about what you have.

Brazil has abundant land and tropical climate — so it exports coffee. Japan has abundant skilled labor and capital — so it exports cars. Countries export goods that intensively use the resources they have in abundance, and import goods that require resources they lack. This simple insight, formalized as the Heckscher-Ohlin model, became the dominant framework for understanding international trade for most of the 20th century.

James Meade then took the analysis further — beyond goods to capital, beyond positive theory to policy, showing how trade and payments interact in a world where money and investment flow across borders.

## Ohlin: Trade Is About Endowments, Not Technology

**Bertil Ohlin (1899–1979)**, a Swedish economist and student of Eli Heckscher, published *Interregional and International Trade* in 1933 — a book that redefined how economists think about why nations trade.

The **Heckscher-Ohlin model** rests on a powerful logic:

- Countries differ in their **factor endowments** — some have abundant labor, others abundant capital, others abundant land or natural resources
- Goods differ in their **factor intensity** — making textiles requires lots of labor, making semiconductors requires lots of capital
- Each country will export goods that intensively use its abundant factor, because those goods are relatively cheap to produce domestically
- Trade thus serves as an indirect exchange of factors: a labor-rich country exports labor (embedded in textiles) and imports capital (embedded in machinery)

Key predictions and extensions of the model:

- **Factor price equalization**: Under free trade, wages and returns to capital tend to converge across countries — even without migration. Trade in goods substitutes for movement of people
- **Stolper-Samuelson theorem** (developed by Samuelson from Ohlin's framework): Trade protection benefits the scarce factor. In rich countries, free trade hurts unskilled workers; in poor countries, it helps them
- **Rybczynski theorem**: If a country's endowment of one factor grows (say, through immigration), it will produce more of the good that uses that factor intensively and less of the other

Ohlin also broke new ground by treating international trade as a special case of interregional trade — the same forces that drive commerce between provinces drive it between nations, just with more barriers. This unified perspective was decades ahead of its time.

## Meade: Trade Policy, Payments, and the Second Best

**James Meade (1907–1995)**, a British economist, complemented Ohlin's pure theory with rigorous analysis of trade policy and international capital movements.

His two-volume masterwork *The Theory of International Economic Policy* tackled the hardest questions in open-economy economics:

- **The Balance of Payments** (1951): Analyzed how a country can simultaneously achieve internal balance (full employment without inflation) and external balance (sustainable balance of payments). Meade showed this requires at least two independent policy instruments — foreshadowing the Tinbergen Rule and laying the groundwork for the Mundell-Fleming model
- **Trade and Welfare** (1955): A systematic analysis of when free trade improves welfare and when it doesn't. Meade formalized the **theory of the second best**: if one market distortion can't be removed (say, a tariff imposed by a trading partner), removing another distortion (your own tariff) may actually make things worse, not better. The optimal policy depends on the full set of existing distortions

Beyond trade theory, Meade made lasting contributions to:

- **Customs union theory**: Analyzed when forming a trade bloc helps or hurts — distinguishing between trade creation (good: replacing expensive domestic production with cheaper imports from partners) and trade diversion (bad: replacing cheap imports from outside with more expensive imports from partners). This framework directly informed the design of the European Economic Community
- **International capital movements**: Showed how capital flows interact with trade flows and exchange rates, and how policy must account for both simultaneously
- **External economies and public goods**: Extended welfare economics to account for cases where markets fail — pollution, public goods, externalities — providing the theoretical basis for corrective taxation

## Two Halves of a Complete Picture

Ohlin explained *why* trade happens — the deep structural forces of factor endowments. Meade explained *what to do about it* — how governments should design trade policy in an imperfect world where simple free-trade prescriptions don't always apply.

Their 1977 Nobel Prize was awarded "for their pathbreaking contribution to the theory of international trade and international capital movements."

## Explain It to a Child

> Imagine two kids trading lunch items. One kid's mom always packs lots of fruit (she has a garden), and the other kid's mom packs lots of cheese (she works at a dairy). They trade fruit for cheese, and both are happier. Ohlin explained why this happens — you trade what you have plenty of for what you don't. Meade figured out the tricky part: what if there's a bully who charges a toll for trading? Should you pay it, go around, or team up with other kids to avoid it? The right answer depends on the whole situation, not just one rule.

## 为什么巴西出口咖啡，日本出口汽车？

在俄林之前，主流答案是李嘉图的：国家之间贸易是因为它们擅长制造不同的东西——由技术差异驱动的比较优势。但俄林追问了一个更深层的问题：*为什么*各国擅长不同的东西？他的答案改变了贸易理论的整个基础：关键不在于技术，而在于你拥有什么。

巴西有丰富的土地和热带气候——所以出口咖啡。日本有丰富的技术劳动力和资本——所以出口汽车。国家出口密集使用其丰裕资源的商品，进口需要其稀缺资源的商品。这一简洁的洞见，被形式化为赫克歇尔-俄林模型，成为20世纪大部分时间理解国际贸易的主导框架。

米德则将分析推向更远——从商品到资本，从实证理论到政策，展示了贸易与支付如何在资金和投资跨境流动的世界中相互作用。

## 俄林：贸易关乎禀赋，而非技术

**贝蒂尔·俄林（1899–1979）**，瑞典经济学家，伊利·赫克歇尔的学生，1933年出版了《区际贸易与国际贸易》——一本重新定义经济学家思考国家间贸易原因的著作。

**赫克歇尔-俄林模型**建立在一个强大的逻辑之上：

- 各国的**要素禀赋**不同——有些劳动力丰富，有些资本丰富，有些土地或自然资源丰富
- 商品的**要素密集度**不同——生产纺织品需要大量劳动力，生产半导体需要大量资本
- 每个国家将出口密集使用其丰裕要素的商品，因为这些商品在国内生产相对便宜
- 贸易因此充当了要素的间接交换：劳动力丰富的国家出口劳动力（嵌入纺织品中），进口资本（嵌入机械中）

模型的关键预测和扩展：

- **要素价格均等化**：在自由贸易下，各国的工资和资本回报率趋于趋同——即使没有移民。商品贸易替代了人口流动
- **斯托尔珀-萨缪尔森定理**（萨缪尔森从俄林的框架发展而来）：贸易保护有利于稀缺要素。在富国，自由贸易损害非技术工人；在穷国，自由贸易帮助他们
- **雷布钦斯基定理**：如果一个国家某种要素的禀赋增长（比如通过移民），它将更多地生产密集使用该要素的商品，减少另一种商品的生产

俄林还开创性地将国际贸易视为区际贸易的特殊情况——驱动省际商业的力量同样驱动国际商业，只是障碍更多。这种统一视角领先了时代数十年。

## 米德：贸易政策、国际收支与次优理论

**詹姆斯·米德（1907–1995）**，英国经济学家，以对贸易政策和国际资本流动的严谨分析，补充了俄林的纯理论。

他的两卷本巨著《国际经济政策理论》直面开放经济中最棘手的问题：

- **《国际收支》**（1951年）：分析了一个国家如何同时实现内部均衡（充分就业且无通胀）和外部均衡（可持续的国际收支）。米德证明这至少需要两个独立的政策工具——预示了丁伯根法则，并为蒙代尔-弗莱明模型奠定了基础
- **《贸易与福利》**（1955年）：系统分析了自由贸易何时改善福利、何时不会。米德形式化了**次优理论**：如果一个市场扭曲无法消除（比如贸易伙伴征收的关税），消除另一个扭曲（你自己的关税）可能实际上让情况更糟而非更好。最优政策取决于现有扭曲的全貌

在贸易理论之外，米德还做出了持久贡献：

- **关税同盟理论**：分析了何时组建贸易集团有利、何时有害——区分了贸易创造（好的：用来自伙伴国的更便宜进口替代昂贵的国内生产）和贸易转移（坏的：用来自伙伴国的更贵进口替代来自外部的便宜进口）。这一框架直接影响了欧洲经济共同体的设计
- **国际资本流动**：展示了资本流动如何与贸易流动和汇率相互作用，以及政策必须同时考虑两者
- **外部经济与公共品**：将福利经济学扩展到市场失灵的情况——污染、公共品、外部性——为矫正性税收提供了理论基础

## 一幅完整图景的两半

俄林解释了贸易*为何*发生——要素禀赋的深层结构性力量。米德解释了*该怎么办*——在简单的自由贸易处方并不总是适用的不完美世界中，政府应如何设计贸易政策。

他们1977年的诺贝尔奖授奖词为："因其对国际贸易理论和国际资本流动的开创性贡献。"

## 讲给小孩听

> 想象两个小朋友交换午餐。一个小朋友的妈妈总是装很多水果（她家有果园），另一个小朋友的妈妈总是装很多奶酪（她在奶牛场工作）。他们用水果换奶酪，两个人都更开心了。俄林解释了为什么会这样——你用自己多的东西换自己缺的东西。米德则想明白了更棘手的部分：如果有个小霸王收"过路费"才让你交换呢？你该付钱、绕路、还是和其他小朋友联合起来避开他？正确答案取决于整个情况，而不是一条简单的规则。
